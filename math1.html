Take any two-digit number. Add the two digits. Write the answer as (first digit) (sum) (second digit). For example:

52 → 5 + 2 = 7 → Answer: 572

2. The Reversing 1089 Trick

We were exploring special numbers online and discovered this fun trick involving 3-digit numbers and a surprising result—1089!

🔹 How It Works

  • Choose any 3-digit number where the first and last digits differ by at least 2. (e.g., 521)
  • Reverse the digits: 125
  • Subtract the smaller from the larger: 521 - 125 = 396
  • Reverse the result: 693
  • Add them: 396 + 693 = 1089

🔹 Why Does It Fail Sometimes?

This process fails when the difference between the first and last digits is less than 2. The math behind why that happens is something we’re still curious to understand!

3. Why Does Dividing by 0 Break Math?

🔹 Division as Intuition

Division has always felt intuitive—like asking, "How many times does one number go into another?" For example, 12 ÷ 3 just means how many times 3 goes into 12 (which is 4). But what about 12 ÷ 0? That would mean how many times 0 goes into 12. That question doesn’t make any sense!

🔹 A Misleading “Proof” that 1 = 2

Some people try to "prove" wrong things using division by zero. Here's one such fake proof:

Let a = b
Then a² = ab
a² - b² = ab - b²
(a - b)(a + b) = b(a - b)
Canceling (a - b): a + b = b
Since a = b: b + b = b → 2b = b → 2 = 1 ❌

Why is this wrong? Because you’re dividing both sides by (a - b), which is zero. That breaks the rules of math!

🔹 Final Thoughts: The Fragility of Math Rules

So from both intuitive and mathematical views, dividing by 0 leads to undefined or broken logic—and that’s why it’s simply not allowed in math!
