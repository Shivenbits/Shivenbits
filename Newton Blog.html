Phase 01  // Propulsion

Rocketry & the Three Laws

A rocket elegantly demonstrates all three of Newton's laws. Firstly, the Third Law comes into play during launch: as the engine combusts fuel, it expels exhaust gases downward at high pressure. These gases push back on the rocket with an equal and opposite force, propelling it upward.

As fuel is consumed and leaves the vehicle, the rocket becomes lighter, meaning less force is required to maintain acceleration. Since the mass reduces while the thrust remains constant, acceleration actually increases over time—a direct application of the Second Law ($F=ma$).

Once the rocket reaches the vacuum of space and the engines cut off, there is no friction or air resistance to slow it down. It will continue moving in the same direction at a constant velocity unless acted upon by an outside force, like the gravity of a celestial body. This is the First Law: the principle of inertia.

1st Law — Inertia 2nd Law — $F = ma$ 3rd Law — Action & Reaction

Phase 02  // Orbital Mechanics

The Weightless State

Astronauts float not because gravity has vanished, but because they are in a state of perpetual free-fall. At the altitude where the ISS orbits, gravity is still about 90% as strong as it is on Earth's surface.

The station doesn't fall to the ground because its horizontal velocity matches the curve of the Earth; it literally falls "around" the planet. Because the astronauts are falling at the exact same rate as the station itself, there is no relative force pressing them against a floor, creating the sensation of weightlessness.

This is the First Law in a curved path. Without gravity acting as a constant external force to bend their trajectory, the crew would fly off into the void in a perfectly straight line.

1st Law — Inertia in orbit Free fall = weightlessness

Phase 03  // Velocity Dynamics

Lowering Orbit

When a satellite moves to a lower orbit, it must move faster to avoid crashing. This is a consequence of Newton's Second Law and his Law of Universal Gravitation. Orbital speed effectively balances the inward pull of gravity:

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
$G$ = gravitational constant  ·  $M$ = Earth's mass  ·  $r$ = orbital radius

As the radius ($r$) decreases, the required velocity must increase. Because gravity is stronger closer to Earth, the satellite must travel at a higher speed to generate the necessary "outward" momentum to maintain its orbit.

2nd Law — $F = ma$ Universal Gravitation

Phase 04  // Gravitational Pairs

Action and Reaction

Newton's Third Law states that the Moon pulls on the Earth with a force equal to that which the Earth pulls on the Moon. Gravity is a mutual interaction between two masses:

$$F = G \frac{m_1 \, m_2}{r^2}$$

We do not notice the Earth moving significantly because its mass is roughly 81 times greater than the Moon's. Since acceleration is inversely proportional to mass (Second Law: $a = F/m$), Earth's vast inertia makes its movement negligible, while the same force causes the Moon to accelerate significantly.

3rd Law — Force pairs 2nd Law — $a = F/m$
